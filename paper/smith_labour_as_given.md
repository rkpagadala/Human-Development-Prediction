# Labour as Given: Adam Smith's Unexamined Assumption and the Two-Hundred-and-Fifty-Year Error It Founded

**Krishna Pagadala**

*Working paper, 2026*

---

## Abstract

Adam Smith published *The Wealth of Nations* in 1776 surrounded by one of the most extensively educated workforces in the world — a product of 250 years of Protestant parish-school investment rooted in Knox's Scottish Reformation. He theorized what he observed: division of labour, specialization, capital accumulation. He did not theorize what made it observable: the literacy and numeracy of the workforce that made division of labour possible in the first place. This omission — labour taken as a natural given rather than a produced input — is the founding error of economic development theory. The error propagated: Marshall and the neoclassicals inherited labour-as-input; Becker (1964) formalized education as a downstream return on prior income; development economics sequenced growth before education; Sen (1989) rerouted the fiscal channel without identifying the mechanism. The empirical record contradicts Smith's implicit causal model. Across 187 countries and six decades of data (parental cohorts from 1950, child cohorts through 2015), education predicts GDP one generational interval (PTE) forward with a positive fixed-effects coefficient; GDP predicts education forward with substantially lower explanatory power. A 28-country long-run panel (1900–2015) confirms: parental lower-secondary completion predicts the next generation's at near-unity (FE β=0.960). Every country that achieved sustained development in the twentieth century built secondary education when it was poor, not after it became wealthy. The causal arrow runs from education to productivity to income — not from income to education. Smith's pin factory ran because Knox had built the schools. The tradition Smith founded has been forgetting the schools for 250 years.

**Keywords:** Adam Smith; Protestant Reformation; Knox; human capital; development economics; PTE; Becker; Sen; causal identification; educational rupture

## 1. The Question Smith Did Not Ask

Adam Smith opens *The Wealth of Nations* with a pin factory. Ten workers, each performing a specialized task — one draws the wire, another straightens it, a third cuts it — produce 48,000 pins per day. The same ten workers, each making pins independently from start to finish, could produce perhaps 200. Division of labour multiplies productivity by a factor of two hundred and forty.

The workers can read. They can understand written instructions. They can be trained to a specification. They can learn new tasks and transmit what they have learned. They are capable of the sequential reasoning that lets a person make pin-heads and then package pins without confusion, the numerical literacy that lets a foreman record output and calculate wages, and the contractual literacy that lets an employer bind workers to a task through a wage agreement they understand. Smith treats all of this as a natural property of labour. Labour is a factor of production. It arrives at the factory door capable of specialization. The question of how it acquired this capability does not arise.

This is not an oversight about detail. It is the foundational assumption of everything that follows. Division of labour requires literate, numerate workers capable of contractual and procedural understanding. Capital accumulation requires merchants who can read accounts and creditors who can enforce contracts. The market mechanisms Smith theorizes — price signals, competition, the invisible hand — presuppose participants capable of reading prices, comparing them, writing agreements, and calculating profit. Smith's entire analytical apparatus depends on a population that has already been educated. He never asks where that population came from.

The answer, had he asked, was directly available to him. He was Scottish. He was born in Kirkcaldy in 1723, educated at Kirkcaldy Burgh School, then at Glasgow University. He had spent his entire life in a country that had operated a statutory system of parish schools since 1696 — itself the legal codification of an educational programme that John Knox had initiated in 1560. The workforce Smith described was the product of 150 years of deliberate, publicly mandated educational investment rooted in a specific theological imperative. He observed its outputs and theorized them as if they were natural.

This paper argues that Smith's unasked question is the founding error of economic development theory: the most consequential omission in the history of a discipline.

## 2. The World Smith Stood Inside

### 2.1 Scotland in 1776

By the middle of the eighteenth century, Lowland Scotland had one of the highest literacy rates in the world. Houston (1985) estimates male literacy in Lowland Scotland at approximately 65% by 1750, based on signature evidence from legal documents — itself a lower bound, since many who could read could not write. In Edinburgh and the other burghs, male literacy was higher still. Female literacy, while substantially lower, was rising. The rural Highlands and the Catholic areas of the northwest were different — less schooled, more oral, more Gaelic — but the Lowlands, where Smith lived and worked and drew his observations, were distinctively and deliberately literate.

This literacy was not ancient. It was the product of a specific historical intervention that had begun less than two centuries before Smith was born.

### 2.2 Knox and the Educational Imperative

John Knox returned from Geneva to Scotland in 1559 carrying Calvin's model of Reformed Christianity. Calvin's Geneva had instituted a school in every parish — education as a civic and religious obligation, not a luxury. Knox adopted this model wholesale. His *First Book of Discipline* (1560) is explicit: every parish in Scotland should maintain a school, every child should be taught reading and arithmetic, the church should fund it and the state should enforce it. The argument was theological: a Christian who could not read Scripture could not be a full participant in the Protestant covenant. The literacy imperative was not a welfare measure. It was a requirement of salvation.

The *First Book of Discipline* was never formally ratified by the Scottish Parliament — the Catholic nobility blocked it. But it shaped educational practice nonetheless. Parish schools multiplied through the late sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries, funded variously by kirk sessions, local heritors, and burgh councils. The *Education Act of 1696* was the legal codification of what Knox had begun: every parish in Scotland was required by statute to maintain a school, with the schoolmaster's salary to be paid by the heritors (local landowners). This was not aspirational policy. It was enforceable law, and it was enforced.

Smith was born into the twenty-seventh year of this statutory system. His father, a customs controller, was literate and professionally educated. His mother, Margaret Douglas, was the daughter of a landed family. Kirkcaldy, his birthplace, was a burgh with its own school — better resourced than the rural parish schools, designed for the sons of merchants and professionals. Smith attended this school from approximately age three, then proceeded to Glasgow University at fourteen, then to Oxford as a Snell exhibitioner. His entire educational biography is a product of the Knox-derived system operating exactly as intended: identifying able children in literate communities and advancing them through a structured pipeline.

The craftsmen Smith observed in Kirkcaldy — the weavers, the smiths, the nail-makers — were operating in a world shaped by the same system. Not all of them had been to school; rural literacy rates were patchy. But the organisational structures of their trades — the guild records, the wage agreements, the written instructions for complex processes, the merchant credit that funded the materials — all depended on a literate stratum that the parish school system had produced and that Protestant religious culture had normalized.

### 2.3 The English Chain

Smith was writing for a British audience, and the English context also matters — though it is different in character. England's Reformation, unlike Scotland's, was political rather than doctrinal. Henry VIII broke with Rome for dynastic reasons; the theology followed. The literacy imperative did not flow from a Calvinist church-school programme as it did in Scotland. What it flowed from was William Tyndale.

Tyndale was an Erasmian scholar who had encountered Luther's ideas at Cambridge and became convinced that the English New Testament had to be put into the hands of ordinary readers. He translated it in 1526, in Antwerp, against the explicit opposition of the English church hierarchy. He was betrayed, arrested, and burned at the stake in 1536. The King James Bible of 1611, the text that shaped English Protestant culture for three centuries, is approximately 83% Tyndale's words. The injunction to read Scripture directly — the core Protestant literacy imperative — entered English culture through Tyndale's translation and the century of nonconformist religious culture that followed it.

This English literacy tradition was less institutionally structured than Scotland's. There was no 1696 Act. The English charity schools and grammar schools were patchier, more dependent on local endowment and voluntary effort. But the Protestant cultural norm — that literacy was a religious virtue, that every household should contain a Bible that the household could read — shaped English popular culture throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. The combination of this cultural norm with the expanding print trade produced, by Smith's time, an English reading public that was qualitatively unlike anything that had existed before the Reformation.

### 2.4 What Smith Could Have Noticed

Smith was not unobservant. *The Wealth of Nations* is full of comparative observations — he notes differences between English and French agriculture, between urban and rural wages, between the productivity of free and enslaved labour. He had read widely in travel literature and knew that much of the world did not operate through the market mechanisms he was theorizing. The pin factory was an English and Scottish phenomenon. He knew this.

What he did not do was ask why. Why did England and Scotland have literate, numerically competent workers capable of the division of labour he described, while most of Asia, Africa, and Latin America did not? The differential was in front of him in the literature he read. He did not theorize it.

The answer — Protestant parish schooling, operating for 150 years in Scotland and less structurally but culturally in England — was the most plausible explanation available. But Smith was inside the system. The fish does not theorize the water. The Scottish Enlightenment — Hume, Ferguson, Hutcheson, and Smith — was itself the product of the Knox-derived educational pipeline. These were men who had been shaped by the parish school and the university system it fed. They could not easily step outside it to identify it as a specific, contingent, historically produced outcome rather than a natural feature of a well-governed society.

## 3. The Error Made Explicit

Smith did not ignore education entirely. Book V of *The Wealth of Nations*, on the revenue of the sovereign, contains a famous discussion of public institutions, including schools. In it, Smith argues that the division of labour, though the source of national wealth, degrades the workers who practice it:

> "The man whose whole life is spent in performing a few simple operations, of which the effects are perhaps always the same, or very nearly the same, has no occasion to exert his understanding, or to exercise his invention... He naturally loses, therefore, the habit of such exertion, and generally becomes as stupid and ignorant as it is possible for a human creature to become."

The remedy he proposes is public provision of basic education — enough reading, writing, and arithmetic that workers are not "mutilated and deformed" by their labour. The state should fund elementary schools in every parish; the cost would be small; the benefit to social order and military capacity would be substantial.

This is a recognizable argument. In attenuated form, it is still made today: education is what you provide to workers to offset the dehumanizing effects of industrial labour. Smith's version is clear, humane, and exactly backwards.

The error is structural, not incidental. Smith proposes education as a *corrective* for the damage done by the division of labour. But division of labour is only possible because of prior education. You cannot have a pin factory staffed by workers who can follow written instructions, negotiate wages, and organize sequences of tasks, in a society where literacy does not exist. The causal arrow Smith draws — division of labour produces stupid workers; education corrects the stupidity — inverts the actual sequence. Division of labour requires educated workers; education, delivered generationally, produces the workforce that makes the specialization possible.

This inversion is not a casual mistake. It reflects the depth of Smith's blind spot. He is so thoroughly inside the educated-workforce world that he cannot see the education producing it. He observes industrial degradation and proposes education as a remedy because that is the only way education appears to him — as a response to a problem the economy creates, not as the foundation the economy is built on.

The corrective framing had consequences. If education is a welfare measure that corrects industrial damage, it is not a productive investment that precedes industrial development. It is downstream, remedial, and expendable in a fiscal crisis. The institutional attitude toward education that flows from Smith's framing — that it is social infrastructure rather than economic foundation — persists in development policy to the present day.

## 4. The Genealogy of the Error

Smith's unasked question passed forward through the economic tradition, and at each step it was not asked.

### 4.1 Marshall and the Neoclassicals

Alfred Marshall's *Principles of Economics* (1890) formalizes labour as a factor of production with given properties. Workers have preferences, skills, and productivity that the market prices. The question of where those skills come from is a question for social history, not economics. The neoclassical framework that Marshall helped establish treats the human endowments that enable economic activity as exogenous — given from outside the economic system, not produced by it.

This is not an oversight unique to Marshall. It reflects the methodological character of neoclassical economics, which abstracts from the historical conditions that produce factor endowments in order to analyze the allocation of those endowments. The abstraction is legitimate for some purposes. But when the abstraction is then applied to questions of development — why some countries are rich and others poor — it imports Smith's founding error directly: it assumes that the factor endowments (including educated labour) exist, and asks how they are deployed, without asking how they came to exist.

### 4.2 Becker and the Formalization of the Error

Gary Becker's *Human Capital* (1964) is the pivotal text. Becker takes education seriously — more seriously than any previous economist in the neoclassical tradition. He models the relationship between education and earnings with precision, treats the decision to invest in education as a rational investment decision, and establishes human capital as a concept with formal theoretical standing.

But the framework is structured in a specific way that encodes Smith's error. Education in Becker's model is an investment that individuals make *from* prior income. The model is of an individual who has resources (or access to credit) and must decide whether to invest those resources in further education or in some other use. The return on the education investment is higher earnings in the future. The logic is downstream: income first, then the decision about whether to invest some of it in education.

This framework cannot represent the fundamental public good character of basic literacy and numeracy — the kind of educational investment that Knox's parish schools delivered. A child in a sixteenth-century Scottish village was not making a rational investment calculation about whether to attend school. The school was there, mandated by statute, and the alternative was illiteracy. The state had made the investment on behalf of the population before any individual income calculation was relevant. Becker's model of human capital investment assumes the institutional conditions for individual choice — including the existing market economy in which educated individuals can receive returns on their investment — without asking what produced those conditions.

The deeper problem is that Becker's model, by framing education as a return on investment, implicitly answers Smith's question in Smith's direction. Education is what you acquire to increase your productivity; productivity generates income; income funds further education. The causal starting point is productivity and income, with education as a response. This is not a distortion of Becker's explicit argument — he is careful about the micro-foundations. But it is the macro reading of his framework that development economics absorbed, and that reading is Smith's causal model in formal dress.

T.W. Schultz, whose 1960 address "Investment in Human Capital" and 1963 book of the same name established the concept independently, is slightly different in emphasis: Schultz is more attentive to agricultural productivity and to the role of education in enabling farmers to respond to technical change. But his framework shares with Becker the essential structure: education as a capital investment, human capital as a productive asset, both downstream of the economic activity that funds them.

### 4.3 The Lewis Model and Unlimited Labour

W. Arthur Lewis's 1954 model of development with unlimited supplies of labour deserves separate attention because it makes Smith's assumption explicit. Lewis's model assumes a subsistence agricultural sector with surplus labour — workers who can be transferred to a capitalist industrial sector without reducing agricultural output. The critical assumption is that this labour supply is unlimited: the pool of potential industrial workers is so large that wages in the capitalist sector are held down by the availability of subsistence-sector workers.

Smith's assumption of educated-labour-as-given appears in Lewis as unskilled-labour-as-unlimited. In both cases, the properties and availability of the workforce are treated as background conditions rather than as variables to be explained. Lewis assumes that transferring peasants from agriculture to industry does not require educational transformation — that the cognitive skills needed for industrial work are either already present or can be acquired on the job without prior investment.

The historical record of industrialization refutes this. The classic English industrial revolution drew on a workforce that had been prepared by a century and a half of growing popular literacy. The industrial towns of northern England — Manchester, Leeds, Birmingham — were in regions of relatively high literacy by eighteenth-century standards. The proto-industrial cottage industries (textiles, metalwork) that preceded factory production required literate record-keeping, contractual relationships, and numerical capacity that illiterate peasants could not provide. Lewis's unlimited labour supply was not literally unlimited — it was constrained by the same educational preconditions that Smith had taken for granted.

### 4.4 Development Economics and the Washington Consensus

The development economics that emerged after World War II — in the work of Rostow, Hirschman, Prebisch, and their successors — mostly inherited the Lewis-Marshall-Becker assumption structure. Education was included in development frameworks, but as social investment or human development rather than as the primary generative mechanism. The sequencing was generally growth first, social investment from the proceeds. The Harrod-Domar model, the dominant framework of the 1950s and 1960s, required physical capital; education was not the binding constraint.

The Washington Consensus that dominated development lending in the 1980s and 1990s — macroeconomic stabilization, trade liberalization, privatization — did not include primary or secondary education expansion as core conditionality. Education was a social sector expenditure, valuable for equity and welfare, but not central to the growth mechanism. This was Smith's causal model, applied through the institutions of the World Bank and IMF to the poorest countries in the world: stabilize first, grow second, invest in social sectors from the proceeds.

### 4.5 Sen and the Inherited Assumption

Amartya Sen's *Hunger and Public Action* (Drèze & Sen, 1989) is a sophisticated and important challenge to income-first development thinking. Sen argues that Kerala, Sri Lanka, and China achieved welfare outcomes far above what their incomes predicted through direct state provision — and that income-first development is neither necessary nor sufficient for human security. This is a genuine advance.

But Sen's critique is made entirely within the framework he inherited from the tradition. He challenges the sufficiency of income; he does not challenge the causal architecture. When Sen asks why Kerala and Sri Lanka achieved high welfare outcomes, his answer is state provision — direct delivery of health services, food distribution, political mobilization of the poor. He does not ask what made the provision effective; he does not ask whether educated populations demand and utilize provision differently than uneducated ones; he does not ask what produced the political capacity of Kerala's poor to demand services. The unasked question — where does the educated population come from? — remains unasked.

Kerala's female literacy was running forty percentage points above the Indian national average decades before the health outcomes Sen attributes to direct provision. Sri Lanka's literacy expansion in the 1940s and 1950s preceded the mortality decline Sen credits to support-led policy by a full generation. Smith's question, applied to Sen's cases, would have produced different answers than Sen's framework provides. The mechanism was education. The provision worked because the population was educated. Sen described fiscal routing; the mechanism was upstream.

## 5. The Evidence: 187 Countries, 1950–2015

The argument so far has been historical and textual. The data from the twentieth and twenty-first centuries provides the causal test that Smith could not have performed but that the tradition he founded should have demanded.

### 5.1 Education Predicts Income; Income Does Not Predict Education

Across 187 countries from 1950 to 2015 (parental cohorts from 1950, child cohorts from 1975), using WCDE v3 data on lower-secondary completion rates for the 20–24 age cohort matched to World Bank GDP per capita data, the within-country relationship between education and development outcomes is clear and asymmetric. Country fixed-effects regression (removing all time-invariant country characteristics) finds:

- Parental lower-secondary completion predicts the next generation's completion at PTE distance (one generational interval, ~25 years): FE β=0.482, R²=0.455
- GDP per capita at T predicts lower-secondary completion at T: FE R²=0.256

The parental-education advantage is 1.8× in explanatory power within countries over time. This is not a cross-sectional finding that confounds country characteristics — it is identified from the variation within each country across decades.

More directly relevant to Smith's causal model: education predicts GDP one PTE interval forward, with a positive FE coefficient (β=+0.012 log-points per percentage-point gain in lower-secondary completion, controlling for initial GDP). GDP does not predict education forward across a PTE interval with comparable strength. The causal arrow runs from education to income, not from income to education.

The lag-decay structure of this relationship confirms the mechanism. Education's predictive power for life expectancy decays slowly across 100-year lags — the signal is detectable at one generation (PTE), two generations (GPTE), and three generations (GGPTE), a smooth curve reflecting the overlapping timescales of the full transmission network. GDP's predictive power collapses rapidly beyond contemporaneous lags. The two decay profiles belong to different causal structures: education's slow decay is the signature of a generational transmission mechanism that propagates independently of short-run economic fluctuations; GDP's rapid collapse confirms it is downstream, not upstream.

The long-run panel — 28 countries with data from 1900 to 2015 — finds a within-country generational transmission coefficient of FE β=0.960. A one percentage-point gain in one generation's lower-secondary completion predicts a near-unity gain in the next generation's, compounding forward. This is the PTE mechanism operating at the macroeconomic level across 115 years: the education of each generation shapes the next (PTE), who shape the generation after (GPTE), compounding forward. The mechanism Smith failed to theorize is, empirically, the dominant dynamic in long-run human development data.

### 5.2 The Developing-Country Cases

Smith's error is most visible in the postwar development record — in the cases that most closely parallel the experience of England and Scotland in the early modern period.

South Korea in 1953 was at $1,390 per capita GDP — below the income of many sub-Saharan African countries today. The government that took power after the Korean War made education the unconditional national priority. Not income first, education later. Education as the mechanism through which income would eventually become possible. Lower-secondary completion went from 41% in 1960 to 77% in 1975, 87% in 1980, 98% in 1990. GDP per capita followed, two decades later, precisely as PTE predicts.

Japan in 1872 mandated universal compulsory education before it had industrialized, before it had good schools, before it had trained teachers. The Meiji government issued the Fundamental Code of Education with almost no budget to implement it. The first schools were staffed by barely educated teachers in inadequate buildings. The quality was low; the coverage was high. The first generation of low-quality schools produced the parents who expected education for the second generation, and the teachers who staffed the third-generation schools. PTE compounded the initial investment across three generations.

Bangladesh, Vietnam, Nepal, Bhutan — all achieved development crossings at income levels that the Smith/Becker/Lewis model would consider insufficient. Bangladesh crossed the development threshold in 2014 at $1,159 per capita. It educated first and grew later, following the education-to-income causal arrow that Smith could not see from inside the Kirkcaldy burgh school.

The inverse cases are equally instructive. Qatar at $69,000 per capita is an education under-performer by 3.7 percentage points relative to its parental baseline. Oil wealth, extracted without the labour-intensive industrialization that requires educated workers, does not automatically produce educated populations. Income does not generate education when the income is not generated by an educational process. Smith's assumption works when it holds — when income and education grow together, as they did in industrializing Britain — and fails when the two are decoupled, as in the Gulf states. The decoupling reveals the assumption.

### 5.3 The Natural Experiment

Cambodia under the Khmer Rouge (1975–1979) provides the closest available natural experiment. The regime deliberately eliminated educated adults: teachers, university graduates, professionals were targeted for execution. The parental education stock of an entire national cohort was physically destroyed. If Smith's causal model were correct — if labour's properties are given by the economy rather than produced by prior education — the destruction of educated adults should have minimal effect on subsequent economic productivity once the economy was reconstructed. If the PTE mechanism is correct, the destruction of one generation's educated parents should produce a specific downstream consequence at PTE distance: depressed educational attainment in the generation those parents would have raised.

Cambodia's lower-secondary completion was 10.1% in 1975 and on a modest upward trajectory. After the genocide, it declined to 9.1% by 1985, recovered under Vietnamese administration and post-Paris Accords reconstruction to 35.1% by 1995 on aid-driven school rebuilding — and then stalled. From 1995 to 2010, despite continued external investment in schools and provision, completion plateaued at 31–36%. The children reaching secondary school age during this period were born approximately 1982–1996 — their parents were the Khmer Rouge-era cohort whose education had been destroyed between ages five and twenty. The parental baseline was near zero; their children inherited that baseline. The recovery from 2011 onward corresponds to the children of the post-genocide recovery cohort entering the school system.

Compare Cambodia to Vietnam over the same period: Vietnam started at 20.0% lower-secondary completion in 1960 and reached 80.8% by 2015 without interruption. Cambodia, on a similar pre-1975 trajectory, should have been at 50–60% by 2000. It was at 36%. The gap is the PTE shadow of the genocide: one generation of destroyed parental education, expressing itself in the educational attainment of the next.

This is what Smith's unasked question looks like, answered. The educated workforce is not a natural given. It is a produced input. When you destroy it, you destroy not just the present workforce but the next generation's educational starting conditions. The factory can be rebuilt faster than the parental generation that would have educated the workers.

## 6. What Asking the Question Changes

If Smith had asked where his workers came from, what would development economics look like?

### 6.1 The First-Order Implication

The first-order implication is the causal reversal. Education precedes income in the causal chain; income is a consequence of education, not its prerequisite. This reversal has immediate policy implications: the development sequencing that has dominated international lending and bilateral aid since the 1950s — growth first, social investment from the proceeds — is backwards. You cannot grow your way to an educated population. You can only educate your way to a productive one.

This is not a new observation; Easterlin (1981) asked "Why Isn't the Whole World Developed?" and identified education as the answer. Lutz & Kebede (2018) demonstrated that replacing income with education on the Preston Curve makes the relationship tighter and explains the upward shift of the curve over time. The argument made here is that the reason these insights have not displaced Smith's framework is that Smith's framework is not just an empirical claim about what drives development — it is a foundational assumption embedded so deeply in the structure of economics that it is rarely visible as an assumption at all.

### 6.2 The Becker Correction

Becker's human capital framework needs to be inverted, not abandoned. The correct model is not individual investment in education downstream of prior income; it is intergenerational public investment in education that enables the individual income streams from which further investment can flow. The initiating investment — the parish school, the Meiji compulsory education ordinance, the post-war Korean education push — is a public commitment made before the returns are visible. It cannot be modeled as an individual rational investment because the individual making it (Knox, the Meiji government, Park Chung-hee) cannot capture the returns — they accrue to the next generation and the generation after that. It is a public good in the strict sense: non-excludable, non-rival within the generation that benefits, and not susceptible to individual cost-benefit calculation.

Becker's framework correctly describes what happens after the first investment: individuals with educational access making decisions about further investment. But it cannot describe the first investment, because the first investment precedes the income that would fund it in any individual rational-choice model. Smith's observation that educated workers are more productive is correct. His implicit sequencing — economy first, educated workers as a product — is wrong. The economy that Smith described was possible because the parish schools came first.

### 6.3 The Sen Correction

Sen's support-led security cases, re-examined through the question Smith did not ask, show that education was the mechanism in every case. Kerala's health gains were produced by an educated population demanding and effectively using health services, not by health services delivered to an uneducated population. Sri Lanka's mortality transitions followed its literacy transitions by a generation. Cuba in 1961 ran a mass literacy campaign as the mechanism for everything that followed — and Cuba was already at 40.3% lower-secondary completion before the campaign, meaning the educational base for rapid gains was already present.

Sen's dichotomy — growth-mediated versus support-led — describes the fiscal routing of development investment, not its mechanism. Both routes work where the educational base is present; neither route sustains development where it is not. Uganda received substantial post-independence health provision investment and achieved welfare gains in the 1960s and 1970s. When Amin destroyed the provision, the gains reversed — because there was no educated population maintaining the demand for, and effective use of, health services independently of the state. Kerala's welfare gains survived decades of political and economic turbulence in India because they were rooted in an educated population that reproduced those gains through household decisions regardless of state provision. Sen did not identify this distinction; it is the distinction that asking Smith's question would have produced.

### 6.4 The Policy Implication

The policy implication of asking the question Smith did not ask is precise: the binding constraint on development in countries with very low female lower-secondary completion is not income, not health provision, not institutional reform. It is the parental education base. At low completion levels, PTE still advances the system forward, but slowly, against the headwind of high fertility and rapid population growth. Fertility declines continuously as female secondary completion rises; sustained state investment is required throughout — the Korea model of steady 10–14 percentage-point gains per five years from 25% to 95% with no inflection and no point at which investment becomes optional.

Development aid and bilateral donor allocation that funds health provision, income transfer, and primary education at the expense of lower-secondary access in low-base countries is making Smith's error at institutional scale. It is funding the outcomes of education while leaving the mechanism underfunded. The outcomes are real but not durable; they depend on continued external provision rather than on an educated domestic population generating them from within.

## 7. Conclusion: The Parish School and the Pin Factory

John Knox wrote the *First Book of Discipline* in 1560. Its central educational proposal — a school in every parish, every child taught to read, the church and state funding it together — was not implemented fully in Knox's lifetime. It took 136 years, until the Education Act of 1696, for it to become statutory law. By 1723, when Smith was born, it had been operating for 27 years. By 1776, when *The Wealth of Nations* was published, it had been operating for 80 years. The workers in Smith's pin factory were, in the statistical sense, the grandchildren of Knox's first generation of parish school pupils. The PTE mechanism had compounded across three generations before Smith observed its output.

Smith described the pin factory. He did not describe the parish school that made the pin factory possible. This is the founding error. Not a failure of data — Smith had access to the history of Scottish education. Not a failure of intellect — Smith was among the most capable analysts of his age. It was a failure of the kind that only proximity produces: the inability to see as a contingent, produced outcome what one has only ever experienced as a natural background condition.

The error propagated because it was embedded, not argued. Smith did not claim that educated workforces arise spontaneously; he simply assumed it and moved on. Marshall did not reject education's importance; he placed it outside the model. Becker did not deny that initial literacy was a social investment; he modeled individual returns downstream of it. Development economics did not oppose education; it sequenced it wrong. Sen did not miss education; he described its outputs without identifying its causal priority. At each step, the unasked question was not asked because the person not asking it could not see that there was a question.

The question is now visible. Across 187 countries and six decades of panel data, education predicts income one PTE interval forward, not the reverse. Every country that achieved sustained development built its secondary education system when it was poor. The natural experiment of Cambodia shows that destroying the parental education stock destroys the next generation's educational starting conditions in a way that provision cannot repair. The evidence is consistent, longitudinal, and identified from within-country variation — the strongest non-experimental evidence available at generational timescales.

The pin factory ran because Knox built the schools. The parish school was not a welfare measure for workers damaged by specialization. It was the precondition for specialization. It was the answer to the question Smith did not ask.

Two hundred and fifty years later, development policy continues to fund the pin factory and underfund the parish school. The founding error has been expensive.
