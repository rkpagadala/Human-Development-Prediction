# Country Action Guide

*Threshold-specific sequences for governments and philanthropists. What to do next depends on where you are.*

---

## How to Use This Guide

Find the band that matches your country's current female lower-secondary completion rate (the percentage of women aged 20–24 who have completed lower secondary school). Then follow the sequence for your band.

The data comes from WCDE v3 (189 countries, 2025 estimates). If you do not know your country's rate, the World Bank's EdStats database and the UNESCO Institute for Statistics both carry recent estimates. Use the 20–24 age cohort, not the enrollment rate — completion is what compounds through Parental Transmission of Education (PTE), not enrollment.

---

## Band 1: Below 25% female lower-secondary completion

*Countries currently in this band include parts of sub-Saharan Africa (Mali, Niger, Chad, South Sudan, Central African Republic). This is the deepest intervention zone.*

**What is happening:** PTE is operating at extremely low base. Each generation of educated mothers is small. Extended family and sibling channels are weak because few adults in the household have secondary education. The automatic trajectory is slow — without deliberate intervention, crossing 40% may take 50–70 years.

**The constraint:** It is not money. Countries in this band often receive more aid per capita than Band 3 countries. The constraint is the absence of secondary teachers, the absence of safe schools within reach of rural girls, early marriage as the competing option, and a parental generation that largely did not complete secondary and therefore does not transmit secondary education as a norm.

**The sequence:**

1. **Begin teacher training for secondary, now.** Secondary teachers require tertiary education. In Band 1 countries, tertiary-educated adults are a thin cohort. The teacher pipeline must start before secondary demand peaks — or it will not exist when you need it. Identify the students completing primary who have the academic capacity for a teacher-training pathway. Fund their tertiary education explicitly and conditionally on returning to teach secondary in rural areas.

2. **Build lower-secondary schools within 3km of every rural community.** Distance is the first barrier to girls' secondary attendance in low-base countries. The school does not need to be good. It needs to exist and be reachable without overnight travel. Low-quality schools that girls attend produce educated mothers. Unbuilt schools produce nothing.

3. **Remove cost barriers at the lower-secondary level.** Fees, uniforms, books, and opportunity costs (girls working at home) are the marginal deterrents in communities where secondary completion is not yet the norm. Conditional cash transfers, uniform subsidies, and fee waivers specifically for girls at the lower-secondary level have documented effects on completion in countries at this band.

4. **Enact and enforce a minimum age of marriage of 18.** Early marriage is the primary competing option for girls in Band 1 countries. It is not primarily a cultural preference — it is an economic calculation made by families who see no future for an educated daughter that compensates for the cost of keeping her in school. The marriage-delay effect of lower-secondary completion is substantial and well-documented. The law changes the calculation; the schools change the alternative.

5. **Measure completion, not enrollment.** A girl enrolled but not completing generates no PTE signal. The outcome that matters is whether she finishes. Build the tracking system now — every child, every school, completion at the end of the year confirmed by an independent check. Countries that achieved Band 3 within 25 years all had enrollment-to-completion tracking systems.

**Expected horizon:** With sustained effort, Band 1 countries can reach 40% female lower-secondary completion in 20–30 years. Without intervention, the same crossing takes 50–70 years. The difference is approximately one full generation of mothers — 25 million to 50 million additional women completing lower secondary in the faster scenario, with the cascading fertility, child-health, and economic effects that follow from each educated mother.

## Band 2: 25–40% female lower-secondary completion

*Countries in this band include Uganda (~32%), Tanzania (~35%), Zambia (~38%), Pakistan (~30%), Afghanistan (~20–30% depending on region). This is the critical threshold zone.*

**What is happening:** PTE is advancing. The parental generation has a meaningful minority of educated mothers. Extended family channels are activating — aunts, older sisters, grandmothers with some secondary education are beginning to shift household expectations. The automatic trajectory is faster than Band 1, but the demographic conditions that make progress hard — high fertility, rapid population growth, thin household resources per child — have not yet eased.

**The demographic context:** Fertility declines continuously as female secondary completion rises; the steepest demographic headwinds are at the lowest completion levels. There is no precise threshold above which the state's work is done. Korea expanded steadily from 25% to 95% under sustained state commitment throughout — 10–14 percentage points gained per five years with no deceleration at any point. The 40% level is a rough midpoint of the fertility transition, not a special inflection point, and crossing it is not a signal to ease investment.

**The constraint:** In Band 2, the school supply problem is partially solved — schools exist. The binding constraints are now: (a) the last-mile activation problem (families who have not yet internalized secondary as normal), (b) secondary teacher availability lagging secondary demand growth, (c) quality sufficient to keep girls enrolled through completion rather than dropping out after a year.

**The sequence:**

1. **Attack the completion gap, not the enrollment gap.** Band 2 countries often have reasonable enrollment in lower-secondary. What they lack is completion. Girls enroll and drop out — for marriage, pregnancy, family obligations, distance to school past the primary level, teacher absenteeism, or curriculum irrelevance. Track the dropout points specifically. Intervene at the dropout moment, not at enrollment.

2. **Scale the teacher training pipeline.** Secondary enrollment is about to grow. Secondary teachers trained at Band 1 population sizes will be insufficient for Band 2 expansion. Fund teacher training colleges at scale, ahead of demand. The eight-year training pipeline means you must fund teachers now for the secondary expansion that will arrive in 2033–2035.

3. **Begin the upper-secondary and TVET pipeline.** Girls completing lower secondary will want to continue. In Band 2 countries, upper-secondary and vocational options are often urban and scarce. Distributed upper-secondary expansion (not prestigious urban schools, but functional rural options) prevents the Band 1 problem repeating one level up.

4. **Engage parents and community leaders directly.** At 30–38%, secondary completion is visible enough in the community that educated women are present — neighbours, relatives, community members whose lives differ from those who did not complete secondary. This is the PTE mechanism becoming locally observable. Community engagement that makes this visibility legible — what does completing secondary do for a woman's life in this village? — activates demand faster than school construction alone.

5. **Target the hardest-to-reach 20%.** The girls who are not completing lower secondary in Band 2 countries are disproportionately rural, from the poorest households, from ethnic or linguistic minorities, from communities with the highest early marriage rates. The automatic trajectory will bring the easy cases; the deliberate intervention must focus on the cases the automatic trajectory leaves behind.

**Expected horizon:** Band 2 countries with sustained effort can cross 40% in 8–15 years. This is the highest-priority zone for philanthropic capital: the multiplier is largest here because the demographic headwinds are still strong and the marginal investment compresses the transition rather than starting from scratch. But crossing 40% is not the finish line — it is a rough midpoint of the fertility transition. Korea crossed 40% in 1960 and kept investing at full pace for another 25 years until near-universal completion. That is the model: sustained state commitment throughout, with no point at which investment becomes optional.

## Band 3: 40–65% female lower-secondary completion

*Countries in this band include India (~55%), Bolivia (~60%), Cambodia (~48%), Ghana (~55%), Morocco (~52%). This is the acceleration zone.*

**What is happening:** Fertility is falling. The PTE signal is strong — educated mothers exist in sufficient numbers to make secondary completion a household norm in many communities. The automatic trajectory is meaningful; deliberate intervention remains important, though the most acute barriers have eased.

**The constraint:** The binding constraint has shifted from access to quality and upper-secondary transition. Girls who complete lower secondary increasingly want to continue. The capacity of upper-secondary systems to absorb them is often the bottleneck. Teacher quality is now a more important constraint than teacher quantity.

**The sequence:**

1. **Shift the investment target to upper-secondary and tertiary.** Lower secondary is now functioning. The multiplier has shifted: the highest marginal returns are now to completing upper secondary and to tertiary access, particularly in fields (medicine, teaching, engineering) that compound through the workforce. Female upper-secondary completion in Band 3 countries is the PTE mechanism for the next generation.

2. **Invest in teacher quality, not just quantity.** Countries in Band 3 typically have sufficient secondary teachers. The quality of teaching — not just coverage — now affects completion rates. Professional development, classroom materials, teacher accountability systems, and selective expansion of teacher training rather than mass expansion are the right instruments.

3. **Build the tertiary teacher training capacity.** Secondary teachers need tertiary education. In Band 3 countries, the next secondary expansion will require a larger tertiary graduate pool. Fund the universities and teacher training colleges that produce secondary teachers, sized for the secondary system you will have in ten years, not the system you have today.

4. **Eliminate remaining rural and ethnic gaps.** Band 3 countries have achieved meaningful average completion, but the distribution is uneven. Rural-urban gaps and gaps by ethnic or linguistic group are often still substantial. Closing the last gap — bringing the lowest-quintile girls to near-universal completion — is both a justice issue and the last PTE multiplier available at this band.

5. **Export the model.** Countries that have successfully crossed from Band 2 to Band 3 have documented experience — which interventions worked, what the political economy looked like, how teacher training was managed — that is directly applicable to Band 1 and Band 2 neighbours. South-South knowledge transfer in education is underexploited. Vietnam, Bangladesh, and Ethiopia have relevant experience. India has experience at massive scale across highly heterogeneous conditions.

## Band 4: Above 65% female lower-secondary completion

*Countries include most of Latin America, Southeast Asia, most of East Asia, much of the Middle East, and all OECD countries. The primary mechanism is now operating at near-full capacity.*

**What is happening:** PTE is operating at near-full strength. The demographic transition is well underway. The binding constraints are now quality, tertiary access, and upper-secondary completion — not lower-secondary access.

**Focus areas:** Upper-secondary and tertiary completion. STEM education. Gender parity in higher education and the labour market. These are real and important issues. But they are not the mechanism that lifts Band 1 and Band 2 countries. Band 4 country philanthropy and development policy should be focused on Band 1 and Band 2, not Band 4.

| Band 3 | 40–65% | ~40 | ~28 million | 10–20 years (to 65%) |
| Band 4 | Above 65% | ~110 | — | Already above threshold |

The 35 countries in Bands 1 and 2 are home to approximately 42 million girls aged 15–19 not completing lower secondary. These are the girls whose non-completion sets the parental baseline for the generation born in 2040–2050. Reaching them now is the leverage point.

## One Number to Track

Female lower-secondary completion rate for the 20–24 age cohort, updated every five years.

Not enrollment. Not primary completion. Not upper-secondary. Not male rates.

The 20–24 cohort completion rate is the PTE input for the generation born 25 years from now. It is the number that determines the automatic trajectory. It is the number that separates countries on a 50-year path from countries on a 25-year path.

If your country is not tracking this number, tracking it is the first action.
