# What Your Country Can Look Like in 25 Years

*A vision document for leaders of developing nations.*

---

## The Promise This Data Makes

There is a generation of children being born in your country right now.

In 25 years, they will be your doctors, your engineers, your teachers, your administrators. The question is not whether that generation will exist. It is what they will be capable of — and that question is largely decided by decisions made in the next five years, not the next twenty-five.

This is not a metaphor. It is a measurable relationship. Across 189 countries and 140 years of data, the education level of a parent generation predicts the education level of the child generation with near-clockwork regularity. The coefficient is close to one: every percentage point of lower-secondary completion you add to today's cohort produces approximately one percentage point of gain in the next generation — and that generation passes it forward again. Call this **P-25** — the parental transmission of education: the education of parents at time T shapes the education of their children at T+25, who shape their children at T+50, compounding forward with each generation.

You are not managing a school system. You are setting the starting conditions for a compounding process that will run for a century.

---

## What the Fast Cases Actually Did

Korea in 1953 was a country in rubble. Infrastructure destroyed. Half the population displaced. GDP per capita lower than many sub-Saharan African countries today. What the government did next is one of the most consequential decisions in modern history — not because it was expensive, but because it was clear.

They decided that education was not one priority among many. It was the mechanism through which everything else would become possible. That decision — not the money, not the infrastructure, not foreign aid — is what separated Korea from dozens of other post-war nations that received similar external support and did not produce the same outcome.

By 1975, lower-secondary completion had gone from 41% to 77%. By 1990, it was 98%. The generation educated in the 1960s and 1970s became the parents who expected their children to go further. The generation educated in the 1970s and 1980s built the institutions that ran the economy. The generation educated in the 1980s and 1990s became the engineers and entrepreneurs of the Korean industrial surge.

None of that was visible in 1953. All of it was predictable from 1953, if you understood the mechanism.

Japan made the same decision in 1872 — before it was wealthy, before it had good schools, before it had trained teachers. The Meiji government mandated universal compulsory education, including for girls, at a moment when they had almost no resources to deliver it. The first generation of schools was low quality. The teachers were barely educated above their students. That did not matter. What mattered was that the commitment was made and sustained. The first generation of schools produced the teachers for the second generation of schools. The P-25 compounding did the rest.

## What This Means for Your Country

You do not need to be Korea. You do not need to be Japan. You need to look at what Nepal did.

Nepal in 1990 had lower-secondary completion below 10%. GDP per capita of $200. No colonial educational legacy. A landlocked geography, a difficult terrain, ethnic and linguistic fragmentation. By 2015, lower-secondary completion had reached 53% — 18 percentage points above what Nepal's own parental education history would have predicted. Nepal is not a tiger economy. But it is a country where a girl born in 1990 had a fundamentally different life than a girl born in 1965, because governments in the intervening period made education a sustained priority.

Bangladesh at $1,250 per capita. Vietnam at $2,090. Bhutan at $2,750. All of them delivered 16–26 percentage points of lower-secondary education above what their parental baseline predicted. None of them were wealthy. All of them made the choice.

The first schools do not need to be good. They need to exist and they need to be free. A child who attends a low-quality primary school is better positioned than a child who attends no school — not just for their own life, but for their child's life 25 years from now. The compounding starts from wherever you start. The cost of a low-quality school is not low quality in perpetuity. It is a first generation that produces a slightly better second generation. That is the mechanism. It works from any starting point.

## The Specific Chain You Are Triggering

When female lower-secondary completion rises, the following happens — not as a hope, but as a measured sequence across 189 countries:

**In 10–15 years:** Fertility falls. Educated women have fewer children, later. This is the single most reliable relationship in the development data. Each child in a smaller family receives more resources — more food, more parental attention, more years of schooling.

**In 15–20 years:** Child mortality falls. Educated mothers navigate health systems more effectively, vaccinate more consistently, recognise illness earlier. The child survival gains from maternal education are larger than the gains from equivalent spending on health infrastructure alone.

**In 20–25 years:** The next generation's education rises. Not because you built more schools — though you did — but because educated parents expect education, support it, and transmit it as a norm. The demand side of education becomes self-sustaining once the first cohort of educated parents reaches parenthood.

**In 25–30 years:** GDP per capita grows. The educated cohort enters the labour force. Productivity rises. Tax base expands. The fiscal returns to the original investment begin to arrive — at exactly the moment when you need resources to push secondary into tertiary.

You do not need to fund all of this. You need to trigger the first link. The chain runs itself.

## The Cost of Not Acting

Every year you delay is not a year of standing still. It is a year of compounding in the wrong direction.

The children born this year will be the parents of children born in 25 years. If this cohort reaches only primary completion, the children born in 25 years start from a lower base. The automatic trajectory — what happens without intervention — does not stay flat. It moves, but slowly, driven only by whatever educational investment previous generations managed to make.

The gap between the automatic trajectory and the policy-driven trajectory is where your leadership actually operates. Nepal's +18 percentage points above baseline — that gap is decisions. It is teacher training colleges built before demand peaked. It is compulsory education laws enforced. It is girls kept in school past primary. Each percentage point in that gap is a real child with a different life and a different capacity to give the next generation a better start.

The gap does not close on its own. It requires the decision.

## What the Educational Rupture Looks Like in Practice

An educational rupture is not a policy. It is a reorientation.

It means that when your finance minister proposes cutting the education budget to close a fiscal gap, the answer is no — and the answer is no because of a shared understanding that education is the mechanism, not one line item among many.

It means that when your education minister proposes expanding primary in urban areas before rural, the answer is no — because the intergenerational transmission works through mothers, and rural girls who do not reach lower secondary become rural mothers who cannot transmit the chain.

It means building the teacher training pipeline now, before secondary demand peaks, because secondary teachers need tertiary education, and the lead time is a full generation. Countries that waited until secondary demand peaked to start training teachers lost a decade.

It means measuring completion, not enrolment. A child enrolled but not completing does not transmit the generational signal. The outcome that compounds is the child who finishes and becomes a parent who expects their child to finish.

None of this requires wealth. Korea made this decision at $1,390 per capita. Nepal made it at $200. The constraint is not resources. It is clarity about what comes first.

---

## The Question

The data from 189 countries over 140 years reduces to a single question for any leader of a developing nation:

**Are you going to make the educational rupture, or are you going to manage the automatic trajectory?**

The automatic trajectory is not nothing. Countries on the automatic path do improve — slowly, one generation at a time, at the rate set by their current parental education base. That is real progress. It is also the difference between crossing 50% lower-secondary completion in 2040 versus 2070.

The leaders who made the rupture — Meiji Japan, Park Chung-hee's Korea, Lee Kuan Yew's Singapore, post-independence Cuba, Nepal's education ministers of the 1990s — did not know with certainty it would work. They knew the mechanism was sound and they committed to it anyway.

The data now confirms what those decisions produced. The mechanism is available to you. The decision is yours.
