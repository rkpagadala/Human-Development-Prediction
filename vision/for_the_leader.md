# The Most Important Decision You Will Make

*A vision document for leaders of developing nations.*

---

## The Promise

There is a generation of children being born in your country right now. They will become your factory workers, technicians, nurses, teachers, engineers. What they will be capable of depends on whether they are educated — and every year that decision is delayed is a cohort of children who miss it, and whose children inherit that gap.

You already know this from your own family — educated parents produce educated children, and uneducated parents produce uneducated children. What the data shows is that this works at national scale exactly the same way. Once a generation is educated, it stays educated. It lives in households, not in state budgets. Health spending stops working when you stop spending. Income collapses in crisis. Education endures. Once a mother is educated, that shapes her children's lives regardless of what happens to the economy or the government. The state creates the education; families make it permanent.

You are not managing a school system. You are shaping what your country becomes.

---

## What the Fast Cases Did

Korea in 1953 was a country in rubble — poorer than many sub-Saharan African countries today, with widespread malnutrition and disease. The government made education the unconditional national priority and held that commitment for thirty-five years.

They built schools with class sizes of 60–80, ran double shifts, and expanded access faster than demand. Within one generation, Korea went from 25% to 95% secondary completion. The generation educated in the 1950s became the parents who expected more for their children. The generation educated in the 1970s became the engineers and entrepreneurs of the Korean industrial surge. None of that was visible in 1953. All of it was predictable.

Japan made the same decision in 1872 — before it was wealthy, before it had good schools, before it had trained teachers. Cuba made it in 1961. Nepal made it in the 1990s. Bangladesh by 2014 had higher life expectancy and lower fertility than the United States did in 1960. None of them were wealthy first. None of them waited for disease or malnutrition to be solved first — every country that made this decision made it *through* poverty, not after escaping it.

The results are not instant, but they are not invisible either. Within fifteen years of Korea's decision, fertility fell from 5.6 to 3.0 children per woman and life expectancy rose from 56 to 65 years — families got smaller and people lived longer, before Korea was rich. Singapore saw the same. These are changes people experience in their own lives, not statistics in a report. And the enrollment numbers that predict them are visible within two years of the decision.

## It Works From Any Starting Point

A girl born in Nepal in 1990 had a fundamentally different life than a girl born there in 1965 — because governments in the intervening period made education a sustained priority. Nepal was landlocked, fragmented, poor. No special advantages. By 2015, it had outperformed its own history by a wide margin.

Bangladesh, Vietnam, Bhutan — same story. The mechanism has worked under dictatorships and democracies, in colonial and post-colonial settings, in large countries and small ones. The United States built universal secondary through thousands of local school boards between 1910 and 1940. Kerala and Tamil Nadu did it within India's chaotic democracy. Korea did it under a military government. The mechanism does not care how the decision is made. It cares whether it is made.

## What Education Triggers

When female lower-secondary completion rises, everything moves at once. Fertility falls — educated women have fewer children, later, and each child receives more resources. Child mortality drops — educated mothers vaccinate, recognise illness earlier, navigate health systems. The next generation's education rises — because educated parents expect and transmit it as a norm. Income follows — educated workers are productive workers.

Korea and Singapore did not wait for these to happen one at a time. They pushed education and watched all of it arrive together. The same educated cohort that makes better household decisions also builds the political demand for health systems, accountability, and rule of law. It is one mechanism producing every outcome development policy has been trying to fund separately.

---

## The Cost of Not Acting

Every year you delay is a year of compounding in the wrong direction. The children born this year will be parents within a generation. If this cohort reaches only primary, the next generation starts from a lower base — against the headwind of high fertility and rapid population growth.

The Philippines shows what quiet delay looks like. In 1950, the Philippines and Korea had nearly identical secondary completion — around 22–25%. Korea made the decision. The Philippines treated education as one priority among many. Korea reached 95% in 35 years. The Philippines took 65 years to reach 75%. No catastrophe, no war — just decades of not deciding.

## What the Decision Looks Like

Keep every child in school. Universal primary, universal lower secondary, universal upper secondary — and then a substantial share into college. A country that achieves universal primary but neglects secondary has built half the mechanism.

That sounds simple. It is not — because the conventional wisdom says get rich first, everything else follows. That idea has been wrong for two centuries. Nehru built steel mills. China built backyard furnaces. Even Korea's stated goal was industrialization. But the countries that succeeded were the ones that put education first, whatever they called it. The evidence is unambiguous: every country that developed educated first.

It means treating education as the non-negotiable item in every budget negotiation — the floor, not the ceiling. Not because other priorities are unreal, but because education is what makes all of them work. It means building secondary and primary simultaneously — sequential expansion takes fifty years to reach what simultaneous expansion reaches in thirty. It means building the teacher pipeline now, before demand peaks. It means measuring completion, not enrolment — the outcome that compounds is the child who finishes.

This is not a five-year project. Korea held the commitment for thirty-five years across multiple governments. No single leader saw it through from start to finish — but every leader who held the line is part of the story. The decision you make now will be carried forward by your successors, just as you may be carrying forward a predecessor's. What matters is that someone starts and no one stops.

None of this requires wealth. Korea did it at $1,038 per capita. Nepal at $423. The fast-expanding countries allocated 15–20% of government expenditure to education — roughly double the global average. That is the floor. Nearly every country in the world today already has a higher starting point than Korea did — the starting point is there, the decision is not. Every objection has been answered by a country that faced it and acted anyway. Reorient your own priorities, and demand that developed nations reorient theirs: aid that funds health delivery while underfunding secondary education is aid that funds outcomes without funding the mechanism that produces them. The constraint is not resources. It is clarity about what comes first.

---

## The Question

**Every country that developed made this decision once. This is yours.**

Park Chung-hee, Lee Kuan Yew, the Meiji reformers — they did not know with certainty it would work. They committed anyway. They are remembered as the founders of their countries' transformation.

The mechanism is available to you. The decision is yours.
