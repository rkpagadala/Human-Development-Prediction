# Why Adam Smith Never Asked Where His Workers Came From

*The founding error of economic development theory and its 250-year legacy.*

---

## The Question Smith Did Not Ask

Adam Smith published *The Wealth of Nations* in 1776. Chapter 1 describes a pin factory: ten workers, each performing a specialized task, producing 48,000 pins per day instead of the 20 each could produce alone. Division of labour. Specialization. Productivity.

The workers can read. They can follow instructions. They can be trained. They understand measurement, they can organize sequences of tasks, they are capable of learning and transmitting knowledge. Smith treats all of this as a natural property of labour — a factor of production that exists and can be put to use.

He never asked why those workers could do all of that, and why most of the world's workers in 1776 could not.

The answer was 250 years of Protestant educational investment, operating directly in the country where Smith lived and worked. He observed the output and theorized it as if it were a natural given. This is the founding error from which development economics has not yet recovered.

---

## The Chain Smith Stood Inside

**1517, Wittenberg.** Martin Luther nails his theses to the church door. The central Protestant argument — that every Christian must read scripture directly, without priestly mediation — is a literacy imperative. If salvation requires personal engagement with scripture, then literacy is a religious necessity.

**1526, Antwerp.** William Tyndale, directly influenced by Luther, translates the New Testament into English. He is burned at the stake in 1536, but the text survives. The King James Bible of 1611 is approximately 83% Tyndale's words. Two centuries of Protestant scripture-reading culture follow: read it yourself, or your soul is in danger. This is the English literacy tradition.

**The Scottish chain is tighter.** Smith was Scottish. John Knox explicitly modeled the Scottish Reformation on Geneva — Calvin's model of a school in every parish. Knox's *Book of Discipline* (1560) calls for a school in every parish in Scotland, funded by the church. The Scottish Parliament passed the Education Act of 1696, requiring every parish to maintain a school. Smith was born in Kirkcaldy in 1723, educated at Kirkcaldy Grammar School (a parish school), then at Glasgow University. The workforce he observed most directly — the Lowland Scottish craftsmen, merchants, and labourers of the eighteenth century — was the product of 150 years of statutory parish education.

**The dynastic embedding.** The Act of Settlement (1701) guaranteed Protestant succession to the British throne. The Hanoverian dynasty took power in 1714. Smith was born under George I, wrote *The Wealth of Nations* under George III. Protestant-literacy culture was embedded in the monarchy, the church, and the school system throughout his entire life. He was surrounded by its outputs. He never noticed it was an output.

## Smith's Own Error, In His Own Book

Smith was not unaware of education. In Book V of *The Wealth of Nations*, he argues that the division of labour renders workers "as stupid and ignorant as it is possible for a human creature to become" — and proposes that the state fund basic education as a remedy for this industrial damage.

Here is the error made explicit: education as a welfare corrective for industrial harm, not as the upstream input that makes industry possible. Smith had the causal arrow exactly backwards. Education is not what you provide *after* division of labour has made workers stupid. Education is what made division of labour possible in the first place. You cannot specialize without the literacy and numeracy that enable complex sequences of tasks, contractual relationships, accounting, and technical training.

Smith saw the output of two centuries of Protestant educational investment and proposed to correct one of its side effects. He never theorized the investment itself.

---

## The Genealogy of the Error

The error did not die with Smith. It propagated forward through every major tradition in economics.

**Marshall and the neoclassicals** (late nineteenth century): labour is a factor input. Its properties are taken as given for the purpose of economic analysis. Human capital accumulation is a downstream question — what you do with income, not what makes income possible.

**Gary Becker, 1964**: *Human Capital* formalizes the relationship between education and earnings. Education is modeled as an investment that individuals make downstream of economic activity — a return on growth, not a prerequisite for it. The framework is rigorous and influential. It assumes that the decision to invest in education follows from growth that has already occurred. The question of where the first growth came from — the question that requires understanding where the educated workers came from — is not asked.

**Development economics, 1950s–1990s**: the field builds on the neoclassical tradition. Income drives development. Health and education are what you buy once you have income. Direct provision supplements what markets cannot deliver. The sequencing is growth first, education later — an elaboration of Smith's unexamined assumption.

**Amartya Sen, 1989**: *Hunger and Public Action* offers a sophisticated rebuttal of income-first development — but only within the framework inherited from the tradition. Sen argues for direct provision as an alternative route to welfare outcomes. He does not challenge the assumption that welfare outcomes are the targets and education is one path among several. He inherited Smith's unexamined assumption and rerouted the fiscal channel. The mechanism — education as the generative precondition — remains unidentified.

The full genealogy: **Smith (labour as given) → Marshall/neoclassicals (labour as input) → Becker (human capital as downstream return) → development economics (income-first sequencing) → Sen (fiscal routing, not mechanism).**

At each step, the question Smith did not ask was not asked. Nobody in this chain asked what produced the educated workforce. The assumption of its availability — Smith's assumption about Kirkcaldy craftsmen, scaled up to the global level — moved from founding error to theoretical axiom to policy prescription.

## What the Data Shows

Across 187 countries from 1950 to 2015, the education level of one generation predicts the next generation's education through Parental Transmission of Education (PTE) with a fixed-effects coefficient of 0.482–0.960 (0.482 in the 187-country panel; 0.960 in a 28-country long-run panel from 1900 to 2015). Education predicts GDP growth 25 years forward. GDP does not predict education forward with comparable strength.

The causal arrow is not Smith's arrow. It is not Becker's arrow. It runs from education to productivity to income — not from income to education to productivity. Every country that achieved sustained development in the twentieth century did so by building secondary education when it was poor, not after it became wealthy.

Japan in 1872. Korea in 1953. Vietnam in the 1980s. Bangladesh in the 1990s. None of them were wealthy first. All of them educated first.

The pin factory ran because Knox had built the schools. Smith observed the factory and forgot the schools. The tradition he founded has been forgetting them for 250 years.

---

## The Question That Changes Everything

The question Smith did not ask is simple: *Where did those workers come from?*

The answer is: from generations of deliberate educational investment, beginning with a religious-literacy imperative, implemented through parish schools funded by church and state, maintained across 150 years before Smith ever picked up his pen.

Development policy that does not ask this question will continue to fund the outcomes of education — income, health, welfare — while leaving the mechanism unfunded. It will continue to produce interventions that work where the educational base already exists, and fail where it does not.

The question Smith did not ask is the question development policy has not yet answered. It is the founding question for a different kind of economics — one that begins not with the pin factory, but with the parish school.
