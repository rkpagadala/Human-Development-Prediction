# China Education History: A Data-Driven Re-examination

*Source: WCDE v3 cohort reconstruction, 1870–2015. All figures are completion rates
for the 20–24 age group (the cohort that passed through the relevant schooling years).*

---

## The Standard Narrative — and Why It Needs Scrutiny

The dominant account of the Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) in Western and Chinese urban
discourse treats it as an unambiguous educational catastrophe: universities closed, teachers
persecuted, the 'ten lost years' that set China back a generation. That narrative captures
something real — but it captures the experience of one slice of Chinese society.

Gao Mobo (2008, *The Battle for China's Past*) makes a different argument: the Cultural
Revolution narrative is constructed primarily from the perspective of the urban educated
elite — the very people whose status was most disrupted. For the rural peasantry, who
comprised roughly 80% of China's population in 1966, the story was different. Rural schools
expanded. Barefoot teachers (赤脚教师) brought basic literacy to villages that had none.
Barefoot doctors (赤脚医生) extended healthcare. Urban-rural inequality in access to
education decreased, not increased.

The WCDE data allows us to test this claim. The cohort reconstruction produces completion
rates for every 5-year birth cohort — reading what each cohort achieved by age 20–24.
The schooling years of each cohort can be precisely dated, letting us identify which
cohorts passed through primary, secondary, and university during the CR years.

## 3. The Cultural Revolution: A Disaggregated Reading

The CR cohorts — those whose schooling overlapped with 1966–1976 — show a split picture
that is invisible if you look only at aggregate 'educational disruption':

| Level | Pre-CR avg gain | CR-era avg gain | Post-CR avg gain |
|:------|:--------------:|:---------------:|:----------------:|
| Primary | 12.9 pp | 6.3 pp ▼ | 1.3 pp |
| Lower Secondary | 6.1 pp | 9.8 pp ▲ | 4.0 pp |
| Upper Secondary | 2.1 pp | 2.8 pp ▲ | 4.8 pp |
| College | 0.7 pp | 0.5 pp ▼ | 3.8 pp |

*▲ = CR cohorts performed better than pre-CR; ▼ = worse*

### What the disaggregation shows:

**Primary education: no scar.** Primary completion grew steadily through the entire CR
period — from 75.1% for the 1970 cohort to 79.6% (1975)
to 85.6% (1980). The 1980 cohort (born ~1958, primary school age
1964–1970, entirely during CR) had HIGHER primary completion than any previous cohort.
This is consistent with Gao Mobo's claim that rural schools expanded, not contracted,
during the CR — 'barefoot teachers' (赤脚教师) were dispatched to villages that had
never had a permanent school.

**Lower secondary: the CR era shows the LARGEST gains in the dataset.**
The 1975 cohort gained 10.6 percentage points over the 1970 cohort;
the 1980 cohort gained 15.0 pp — the largest 5-year jump in the entire
1870–2015 series. These cohorts' secondary schooling fell squarely within the CR.
The expansion was driven by 民办学校 (community-run schools): village-level secondary
schools that the CR actively promoted to replace the 'bourgeois' centralised system.
Their quality was uneven, but they existed where nothing had existed before.

**Upper secondary: a real but partial scar.**
Upper secondary growth nearly stalled between the 1965 (8.8%) and 1970
(8.9%) cohorts — a gain of just 0.09 pp. The 1975 cohort
partially recovered (12.2%, +3.3 pp), and the 1980 cohort
surged to 20.3% as Deng restored and rebuilt
the gaokao pipeline. The 1985 cohort (secondary school 1973–1978, straddling late CR
and early Deng) gained only 1.6 pp. Then the 1990
cohort (secondary school 1978–1983) actually shows a DECLINE to 19.2%
(-2.6 pp), suggesting that the early Deng
period's rebuilding of secondary infrastructure had its own disruptions — schools
were physically present but teacher quality and curriculum were being reconstructed.

**College: the only genuine 'lost decade'.**
University admission was genuinely devastated. College completion for the 1965 cohort
(2.54%) actually FELL for the 1970 cohort (2.42%) and
1975 cohort (2.79%). Universities were closed from 1966–1970 and reopened
on a 'worker-peasant-soldier' (工农兵) admissions basis until 1976. The gaokao (national
entrance exam) was abolished in 1966 and not restored until 1977. Even after restoration,
college rates were minimal — only 5.2% for the 1985 cohort. This
affected approximately 2–3% of the population: those who would have attended university.

**The critical asymmetry**: The college disruption — the 'lost generation' of intellectuals
and professionals — affected 2–3% of the population. The primary and lower-secondary
expansion benefited 80%+ of a population that was predominantly rural and had limited
access to education before 1966. Whether this trade-off was worth it is a values question;
whether the data shows educational catastrophe is a factual question. The data says: no,
not for most Chinese people.

## 4. Life Expectancy and Fertility Through the CR — The Barefoot Doctor Effect

| Period | Life expectancy (e0) | Δ e0 | TFR | Δ TFR |
|:------:|:-------------------:|:----:|:---:|:-----:|
| 1950–1954 | 45.4 | — | 6.061 | — |
| 1955–1959 | 46.3 | +0.9 | 5.792 | -0.269 |
| 1960–1964 | 45.4 | -0.9 | 5.717 | -0.075 | *(Great Leap famine)*
| 1965–1969 | 54.4 | +9.0 | 6.280 | +0.563 |
| 1970–1974 | 58.5 | +4.1 | 5.123 | -1.157 | **◄ CR**
| 1975–1979 | 62.5 | +4.0 | 3.022 | -2.101 | **◄ CR**
| 1980–1984 | 65.5 | +3.0 | 2.734 | -0.288 |
| 1985–1989 | 67.4 | +1.9 | 2.635 | -0.099 |
| 1990–1994 | 68.8 | +1.4 | 1.909 | -0.726 |
| 1995–1999 | 70.8 | +2.0 | 1.545 | -0.364 |
| 2000–2004 | 73.1 | +2.3 | 1.586 | +0.041 |

Life expectancy grew by **+8.1 years** from 1965 to 1980, the exact CR period.
This is one of the fastest e0 gains China recorded in any 15-year window outside of
the post-famine recovery. The barefoot doctor (赤脚医生) programme, launched in 1965
and massively scaled during the CR, trained approximately 1 million village-level
health workers by 1975. They provided vaccinations, basic sanitation education, and
maternal care in areas where no medical infrastructure had previously existed.

For comparison: e0 in 1960 was 45.4 (the Great Leap Forward famine nadir);
by 1965 it had recovered to 54.4; by 1975 it was 62.5;
by 1980 it was 65.5. The CR period added ~11 years of
life expectancy. This is not a record of a society consuming its human capital —
it is a record of one that was investing in the health of its rural majority.

**Fertility: the 'Later, Longer, Fewer' campaign (晚稀少)**

TFR fell from 6.28 in 1965 to 3.02 in 1975 — a decline of
3.26 children per woman. This was NOT the One Child Policy (which
was enacted in 1980). The 晚稀少 campaign, which encouraged later marriage, longer
spacing, and fewer births, was implemented from 1971 under Zhou Enlai — in the middle
of the CR. It was one of the most effective voluntary fertility transitions in history,
reducing TFR by 3.26 before any coercive policy was in place.
Higher female education (expanding primary and lower secondary for girls) and the CR's
gender-equality rhetoric both contributed.

## 5. The Deng Era (1978–2015): What Actually Drove the Explosion?

The standard framing: Deng's market reforms unlocked education. But the data suggests
something more nuanced: the Mao era, including the CR, built the primary foundation
that made the Deng secondary and tertiary expansion possible.

### 5.1 The T-25 Intergenerational Chain

| Child cohort | Child lower_sec % | Parent cohort | Parent lower_sec % | Intergenerational gain |
|:------------:|:-----------------:|:-------------:|:-------------------:|:----------------------:|
| 1975 (Cultural Revolu) | 47.1% | 1950 | 9.9% | **+37.1 pp** |
| 1980 (Cultural Revolu) | 62.1% | 1955 | 17.2% | **+44.9 pp** |
| 1985 (Early Deng (197) | 72.8% | 1960 | 23.8% | **+49.0 pp** |
| 1990 (Early Deng (197) | 75.1% | 1965 | 31.5% | **+43.6 pp** |
| 1995 (Reform accelera) | 80.3% | 1970 | 36.5% | **+43.8 pp** |
| 2000 (Reform accelera) | 86.1% | 1975 | 47.1% | **+39.0 pp** |
| 2005 (Massification e) | 89.8% | 1980 | 62.1% | **+27.7 pp** |
| 2010 (Massification e) | 92.7% | 1985 | 72.8% | **+19.8 pp** |
| 2015 (Massification e) | 94.6% | 1990 | 75.1% | **+19.5 pp** |

The Deng-era cohorts (1980–2015) were the children of Mao-era parents whose primary
literacy was built during the 1950s campaigns and whose lower-secondary access expanded
during the CR via community schools. The T-25 multiplier ran on a foundation that the
pre-reform period laid.

### 5.2 The Four Phases of Deng-Era Education

**1978–1985: Gaokao restoration and secondary rebuild.**
The national university entrance examination was restored in 1977 (first sitting: December
1977, with 5.7 million candidates for 270,000 places). Upper secondary expanded as
families rapidly understood that gaokao preparation required formal schooling. Lower
secondary continued its momentum from the CR era.

**1985–1995: Compulsory Education Law (1986).**
The 义务教育法 made nine years of schooling (primary + lower secondary) compulsory.
This formalised and accelerated what community schools had begun informally during the CR.
Lower secondary went from 62% (1980 cohort) to 80% (1995 cohort).

**1995–2005: Upper secondary acceleration.**
Upper secondary expanded from 23.1% (1995 cohort) to 38.3%
(2005 cohort), driven by economic incentives: a market economy created clear wage
premiums for educated workers that the planned economy had not.

**1999–2015: Massification of higher education.**
The 1999 decision to nearly triple university enrollment (高校扩招) transformed college
from an elite path to a mass expectation. College completion:
  - 1995 cohort: 8.1%
  - 2000 cohort: 11.7%
  - 2005 cohort: 17.5%
  - 2010 cohort: 24.1%
  - 2015 cohort: 36.4%

## 7. What Was Actually Lost: The 'Lost Generation' Reconsidered

The term 'lost generation' (失落的一代) refers specifically to those who should have
attended university in 1966–1976 but could not. The data quantifies this:

| Birth cohort | Expected university entry | College completion | Counterfactual estimate |
|:------------:|:-------------------------:|:-----------------:|:-----------------------:|
| ~1943 | ~1961–1965 | 2.54% | ~4.4% (est.) |
| ~1948 | ~1966–1970 | 2.42% | ~6.3% (est.) |
| ~1953 | ~1971–1975 | 2.79% | ~8.2% (est.) |
| ~1958 | ~1976–1980 | 3.76% | ~10.1% (est.) |

These numbers translate to approximately:
- ~15 million people per birth cohort in the 1960s
- Actual college: ~2–3% = ~300,000–450,000 per cohort
- Counterfactual: ~4–7% = ~600,000–1,000,000 per cohort
- Deficit per cohort: ~200,000–500,000 graduates not produced over 10 years
- Total 'lost' graduates: roughly 2–4 million across the CR decade

These 2–4 million people — the engineers, doctors, scientists, and administrators who
were not trained — represent a genuine and serious cost. The economic literature
(Meng & Gregory, 2002; Bertrand et al., 2021) estimates significant earnings
penalties and career disruptions for CR-affected cohorts.

But this number must be set against:
- A rural primary expansion that added roughly 100–150 million people to basic literacy
- A lower-secondary expansion that added 50–80 million people to the secondary-educated
  workforce over CR-era cohorts
- A life-expectancy gain of 8+ years for 700+ million people

The Cultural Revolution damaged the top of the educational pyramid. It widened the base.
Whether widening the base at the cost of the apex was the right policy is a serious
debate. What is not defensible is treating the urban apex experience as the whole story.

## 8. Synthesis: What Drives China's Educational Trajectory?

### Three forces, not one

| Period | Primary driver | Beneficiary | Data signal |
|:-------|:--------------|:------------|:------------|
| 1949–1966 | Maoist mass literacy campaigns | Entire rural population | Primary: 0% → 60% in 15 years |
| 1966–1976 | Community schools (民办学校); barefoot teachers | Rural lower secondary | Biggest lower_sec gains in dataset |
| 1966–1976 | University closure / worker-peasant-soldier admission | Urban elite (negative) | College stagnation at 2–3% |
| 1966–1976 | Barefoot doctors (赤脚医生); 晚稀少 campaign | Rural health and fertility | e0 +8 yrs; TFR -2.1 in 10 years |
| 1978–1999 | Gaokao restoration; Compulsory Education Law 1986 | Upper secondary and university | Upper_sec 12% → 24%; college 3% → 12% |
| 1999–2015 | University massification (高校扩招) | University | College 12% → 36% in 15 years |

### The Lutz (2009) lens

Lutz argues that education is the root cause of development and income is the
intermediate outcome. China is a partial test of this thesis — but a tricky one,
because China's income growth since 1978 is so spectacular that it tempts the
conclusion that Deng's market reforms are the cause and education is the effect.

The data suggests the causation runs the other way: the primary and lower-secondary
foundation built in the Mao era — including the CR period — created the literate
workforce that could absorb technology, follow factory instructions, and train children
who could pass the gaokao. The Deng economic miracle needed that foundation. A rural
peasantry at 15% primary completion (China's level in 1950) cannot absorb foreign
direct investment into export manufacturing. A rural workforce at 86%
primary and 62% lower secondary (the level when Deng's reforms began) can.

### Gao Mobo's thesis: verdict from the data

Gao's claim that the Cultural Revolution was not the educational catastrophe of
standard historiography is **supported at the primary and lower-secondary levels**.
The WCDE data shows no interruption in primary growth and the largest lower-secondary
gains of the century during the CR years.

Gao's claim is **not supported at the college level**: university completion stagnated
for 15 years, and the loss of 2–4 million graduates across a decade was economically
and socially significant.

The resolution: both claims are true for different populations. The standard narrative
is correct for urban educated families — the 2–3% who aspired to university. Gao Mobo
is correct for rural peasants — the 80% who for the first time got a school within
walking distance of their village.

## 9. China Versus the World During the Cultural Revolution

The "lost decade" framing only makes sense against a counterfactual — what would China
have achieved without the CR? One way to ground that is to ask: compared to what other
developing countries were actually doing at the same time, was China's CR-era record a
catastrophe, a mediocrity, or something else entirely?

China's +30.6 pp gain during the CR ranks **third in the world**, behind only Taiwan and
South Korea — both of which received massive US aid and had authoritarian governments
specifically oriented toward education as an economic strategy. Among countries without
that external financial subsidy, China's CR-era lower-secondary expansion was arguably
the fastest in the world.

India — China's most natural comparator, similar income level in 1965, similarly large
population, democratic governance — gained only +12.1 pp. No Cultural Revolution, no
deliberate destruction of "bourgeois" education. Just slow drift. By 1980, India sat at
30.2% lower secondary completion; China at 62.1%. That 32-percentage-point gap did not
come from China's success after Deng — it opened *during the CR*.

### 9.2 College: The "Stagnation" in Global Context

China's college completion during the CR:
- 1965 cohort: 2.54%
- 1970 cohort: 2.42% (slight fall, universities closed)
- 1975 cohort: 2.79% (worker-peasant-soldier admissions)
- 1980 cohort: 3.76% (gaokao restored 1977)

Now compare that to the developing world in 1975 — countries with no Cultural Revolution,
no deliberate disruption of higher education, simply going about their normal affairs:

| India | 5.6% | 6.2% |
| Egypt | 7.9% | 9.5% |
| Iran | 6.3% | 7.1% |
| South Korea | 4.7% | 6.6% |
| Mexico | 4.8% | 6.2% |

China's 2.8% college completion for the 1975 cohort placed it at roughly the **30th
percentile globally** — below India, Egypt, South Korea, and Latin America, but above
Indonesia, Nigeria (without oil-era expansion), Bangladesh, Ghana, and Cambodia.

**This is stagnation, not catastrophe.** The countries below China in college completion
had no Cultural Revolution. Bangladesh was at 0.9%. Ethiopia 0.7%. Cambodia 0.9%.
These countries were not closing universities — they simply never had an educated
population to attend them. China's "stagnated" college rate was still 3× Bangladesh's
normal functioning rate.

### 9.3 The Counterfactual That Wasn't Asked

The standard critique compares China-during-CR to China-without-CR (an imagined
trajectory). But that counterfactual country does not exist and cannot be observed.
What *can* be observed is China relative to:

1. **Peer developing countries at the same time**: China outperformed most of Asia and
   Africa at the lower-secondary level during the CR by a wide margin.

2. **China's own trajectory before the CR**: Primary and lower secondary continued
   accelerating. The CR did not reverse a prior trend — it continued it at the mass level
   while disrupting the elite level.

3. **What followed**: The Deng-era explosion in secondary and tertiary education was built
   on the literate, partially-secondary-educated workforce that the Mao era — including
   the CR — produced. By 1980, China had 62% lower secondary completion. That is the
   platform from which the world's largest sustained educational expansion was launched.

### 9.4 Whose History Was Being Written

The "lost decade" narrative was constructed by:
- Urban intellectuals who were personally disrupted — sent to the countryside, humiliated,
  sometimes imprisoned
- Western journalists and academics who had access to this group
- Post-1978 Chinese state, which needed to delegitimise the CR to justify Deng's reforms
- The international academic literature which drew on urban memoirs and official statistics
  that emphasised university closures

What was not recorded with equal force:
- Rural communities gaining their first secondary school
- Girls in Sichuan villages learning to read
- Village health workers learning basic medicine
- The cohort data, which was not reconstructed at all until the WCDE series made it possible

The WCDE cohort reconstruction is, in a sense, the first time the rural 80% have a
quantitative voice in this debate. And what they say, in percentage points, is that the
Cultural Revolution era was one of the fastest periods of educational expansion in Chinese
history for the people who had previously had nothing.

That does not make the CR's violence, the persecution of intellectuals, or the destruction
of universities acceptable. It makes the label "educational catastrophe" a category error —
the catastrophe for 2–3% of the population, the expansion for the other 80%.

## Data Notes

- All completion rates are from WCDE v3, cohort reconstruction (script 02b).
- Cohort year = the year the cohort was approximately 20–24 (completed secondary).
- Survivorship bias in pre-1980 data: modest upward bias for education rates, making
  the CR-era gains slightly overstated; direction does not change conclusions.
- e0 and TFR from WCDE SSP2 historical series.
- GDP data not used directly; income context from World Bank background knowledge.
