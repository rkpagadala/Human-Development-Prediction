# China's Education History: A Data-Driven Re-examination

*Analysis based on WCDE v3 cohort reconstruction, 1870–2015. All figures are completion
rates for the cohort measured at age 20–24 — the generation that passed through the
relevant schooling years.*

---

The dominant account of the Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) in Western and Chinese urban
discourse treats it as an unambiguous educational catastrophe: universities closed, teachers
persecuted, the "ten lost years" that set China back a generation. That narrative captures
something real — but it captures the experience of one slice of Chinese society, and it has
been sold, uncritically, as the history of China.

Gao Mobo (2008, *The Battle for China's Past*) argues that the Cultural Revolution narrative
was constructed from the perspective of the urban educated elite — the very people whose
status was most disrupted. His claim is not merely that a different group had a different
experience. It is that the standard narrative mistakes elite history for national history.
China in 1966 was approximately 80% rural peasant. What happened to Chinese society is
first and foremost what happened to that majority. And for that majority — rural schools
expanded, barefoot teachers (赤脚教师) brought literacy to villages that had none, and
urban-rural inequality in access to education decreased, not increased.

The WCDE cohort data makes it possible to test this claim quantitatively for the first time.
The cohort reconstruction reads what each generation achieved in education by age 20–24, and
the schooling years of each cohort can be precisely dated against the historical record. The
result is a picture that is more complicated, and more interesting, than either the standard
catastrophe narrative or a simple rehabilitation of the CR.

---

## The Trajectory

China in 1875 was near zero on every measure of formal education — consistent with the keju
system's design, which optimised for producing a tiny examination-passing elite and had no
interest in mass literacy. The 1905 abolition of the keju opened the door, but the Republican
period (1912–1949) — warlords, Japanese invasion, civil war — prevented any systematic
expansion. By 1950, only 32% of the generation turning 20–24 had completed primary school,
and barely 10% had any secondary education at all.

The People's Republic changed this fast. By the 1960 cohort, primary completion was 60% and
lower secondary had reached 24%. These gains came from the mass literacy campaigns of the
1950s — a deliberate, ideologically driven push to make the peasant population literate.
Whatever else Mao's policies achieved or failed at, the education numbers in this decade are
unambiguous.

Then comes the Cultural Revolution, and the data becomes genuinely surprising.

---

## The Cultural Revolution: What the Data Actually Shows

The key to reading the CR's educational impact is to disaggregate by level. The experience
at university, at high school, and at village primary school were three different stories.

**At the primary level, the CR left no scar at all.** Primary completion grew from 70% for
the cohort that was primary-school age just before the CR, to 76% for those in school at its
onset, to 80% at its peak, to 86% for the cohort whose primary schooling fell mostly within
the CR years. The children of peasant villages were learning to read faster during the
Cultural Revolution than before it. This is exactly consistent with Gao Mobo's account of
barefoot teachers dispatched to villages that had never had a permanent school.

**At the lower secondary level, the CR period coincides with the largest gains in China's
entire 1870–2015 history.** The cohort whose secondary schooling fell in 1970–1975 gained
10.6 percentage points over the previous cohort. The next cohort — secondary school
1972–1977, the peak CR years — gained 15.0 percentage points, the largest single 5-year
jump in the entire dataset. The data does not tell us which specific factors drove this.
CR-era policies documented in historical sources include 民办学校 (community-run schools)
promoted as alternatives to the centralised system, reduced urban migration keeping children
in rural villages, and ideological pressure for mass participation in basic education. Any
or all of these could have contributed. What the data establishes is the timing: the gains
were largest during the CR years, not before or after.

**At the upper secondary level, there is a real scar, though a partial one.** Upper secondary
growth nearly stalled between 1965 and 1970 — a gain of barely 0.1 percentage points over
five years. The disruption to formal high schools was genuine. Recovery came from 1975
onwards as some formal schools reconstituted themselves, and the 1980 cohort (which caught
the late CR and the early gaokao years) posted a jump of +8.1 pp — the largest upper
secondary gain in the data outside of the post-1995 acceleration.

**At university level, the CR produced the only genuine lost decade.** Universities closed
in 1966, reopened in 1970 on a worker-peasant-soldier (工农兵) admissions basis that bypassed
academic criteria, and did not restore merit-based entry until the gaokao was reinstated in
1977. College completion, which had been growing slowly through the 1950s and early 1960s
(reaching 2.5% for the 1960 cohort), stagnated and even fell slightly — 2.54% for the 1965
cohort, 2.42% for 1970, 2.79% for 1975. A rough estimate suggests 2–4 million people who
would have attended university under a normal trajectory did not. These were the engineers,
doctors, scientists, and administrators of a generation — and their absence was a real cost,
documented in subsequent career and earnings data.

The critical asymmetry: the college disruption affected approximately 2–3% of the
population. The primary and lower-secondary expansion benefited the other 80%.

## Setting "Catastrophe" Against the World

The "lost decade" framing only makes sense against a counterfactual — what would China
have achieved without the CR? That counterfactual country cannot be observed. What can be
observed is how China performed relative to other developing countries going about their
normal affairs during the same years.

Among all countries in the WCDE data, China's lower secondary gain from 1965 to 1980
(+30.6 percentage points) ranks **third in the world**. Only Taiwan (+46.5 pp) and South
Korea (+35.7 pp) did more — both running on massive US aid with authoritarian governments
explicitly oriented toward education as economic strategy. Among countries without that
external financial subsidy, China's CR-era lower-secondary expansion was arguably the
fastest on earth.

India is the most instructive comparison. Same income level in 1965, similar population
scale, democratic governance, no Cultural Revolution, no deliberate disruption of any kind.
India gained +12.1 pp in lower secondary over the same 1965–1980 period. By 1980, India
stood at 30.2% lower secondary completion; China at 62.1%. That 32-percentage-point gap
did not open after Deng — it opened *during the CR*, against a country that was simply
drifting along without any forcing mechanism.

As for college, China's "stagnated" rate of 2.8% for the 1975 cohort placed it at roughly
the 30th percentile globally — below India (5.6%), South Korea (4.7%), and most of Latin
America, but above Indonesia (1.7%), Bangladesh (0.9%), Ethiopia (0.7%), and Cambodia (0.9%).
None of those countries had a Cultural Revolution. Bangladesh at 0.9% was simply the normal
baseline for a poor country that had never systematically built university access. China's
"catastrophic stagnation" was still three times Bangladesh's undisturbed normal rate.

The label "educational catastrophe" was written by people who compared China during the CR
to an imaginary China without it. The data compares China during the CR to the actual world
of the 1960s and 1970s. By that comparison, at the level where 80% of the population lived,
it was one of the fastest educational expansions in history.

## Life Expectancy and Fertility: The Barefoot Doctor Effect

Education was not the only human development metric moving in China's favour during the
Cultural Revolution. Life expectancy grew from 54.4 years in 1965 to 62.5 in 1975 to 65.5
in 1980 — a gain of 11 years over the CR period, one of the fastest recorded anywhere
outside of post-famine recovery. The barefoot doctor (赤脚医生) programme, launched in 1965
and massively scaled during the CR, trained approximately one million village-level health
workers by 1975. They provided vaccinations, basic sanitation, and maternal care in areas
where no medical infrastructure had previously existed.

Fertility tells a similarly striking story. The total fertility rate fell from 6.28 children
per woman in 1965 to 3.02 in 1975 — a decline of 3.26 children in ten years — before the
One Child Policy existed. This was the 晚稀少 (later, longer, fewer) campaign, implemented
from 1971 under Zhou Enlai in the middle of the CR. It stands as one of the most effective
voluntary fertility transitions in history, and it happened not because of coercion but
because rising female education and the CR's gender-equality rhetoric shifted norms in
rural communities. A society consuming its human capital does not show these numbers.

The gender data makes this concrete. China's lower secondary cohort completion rates, by sex:

| Year | Both sexes | Female | Female/Both |
|:----:|:----------:|:------:|:-----------:|
| 1950 | 9.9% | 4.0% | 0.40 |
| 1960 | 23.8% | 15.2% | 0.64 |
| 1970 | 36.5% | 26.9% | 0.74 |
| 1980 | 62.1% | 52.4% | 0.84 |
| 1990 | 75.1% | 69.4% | 0.92 |
| 2000 | 86.1% | 83.5% | 0.97 |
| 2010 | 92.7% | 91.9% | 0.99 |
| 2015 | 94.6% | 94.4% | 1.00 |

In 1950, women completing lower secondary were at 40% of the male-and-female average —
a massive gender gap. By 1970, at the start of the CR, it was 74%. By 1990, after two
decades of the CR's gender-equality policies and the early reform era, it was 92%. By
2015 it had closed entirely. The steepest convergence — from 0.74 to 0.92 — happened
across the CR and early Deng decades. Gender parity in lower secondary is not a post-1978
market achievement; the foundations were laid when the CR declared women equal
participants in production and study.

## Did Deng Actually Accelerate Education?

The standard framing positions Deng as the architect of China's educational rise — restoring
universities, reforming the system, and unleashing the expansion that produced the world's
most educated workforce. The data tells a more complicated story.

Looking at the average gain in lower secondary completion per 5-year cohort across eras,
the Cultural Revolution (1965–80) averaged +10.2 percentage points per cohort — the fastest
of any era in the data. Early Deng (1980–90) averaged +6.5 pp, slower than the CR. The
mid-Deng years (1990–00) slower still at +5.5 pp. The lower secondary gains did not
accelerate under Deng. They decelerated.

The 1990 cohort — born 1966–70, doing secondary school under Deng from 1978 to 1984 — tells
the sharpest part of this story. Their lower secondary gain was just +2.3 percentage points,
the worst single period since the Great Leap Forward famine. This cohort was doing secondary
school during Deng's high-growth decade. India, with no reforms of any kind, gained +3.6 pp
in the same period.

Upper secondary under early Deng was worse still. The average upper secondary gain for
1980–90 was negative: −0.51 pp per cohort. The 1990 cohort upper secondary actually fell by
2.6 percentage points from the previous generation. Several factors plausibly contributed:
the Compulsory Education Law of 1986 mandated only 9 years (stopping at lower secondary,
leaving upper secondary to market demand); economic reform created an early-entry wage
option that competed with staying in school; and the transition from one type of school
system to another may have created a supply gap. Vietnam shows the same pattern after Doi
Moi (1986): lower secondary gains flipped negative in 1990 and 1995 before recovering in
2000. The data cannot distinguish between these explanations — but the trough itself is
real and the Deng era owns it.

At the college level, Deng's genuine contribution was restoring the gaokao in 1977 — a
meritocratic selection mechanism that reoriented family investment toward formal education.
That was real and consequential. But the actual college completion numbers moved slowly
across his entire leadership: from 3.8% (1980 cohort) to 8.1% (1995 cohort), a gain of
4.4 percentage points over 15 years.

The inflection that produced modern China's educated workforce was the 1999 大扩招 (university
enrollment expansion) under Jiang Zemin — a policy triggered by the 1997–98 Asian financial
crisis. The explicit rationale was keeping graduates out of a contracting labour market for
four more years while the economy recovered. The fastest tertiary educational expansion in
Chinese history was, in its immediate motivation, an unemployment management tool. Under
Jiang, college completion jumped +9.4 pp in 10 years. Under Deng it had gained +4.4 pp in 15.

## Attribution

The workers who staffed China's export manufacturing economy in the 1980s and 1990s —
the people who made Deng's economic miracle physically possible — were literate and
partially secondary-educated because of Mao and the Cultural Revolution, not because of
Deng. The human capital stock that absorbed foreign direct investment, followed factory
instructions, and trained the next generation through the T-25 multiplier was built in
the 1950s literacy campaigns and the CR period. A rural workforce at 15%
primary completion (China's level in 1950) cannot sustain export manufacturing. At 86%
primary and 62% lower secondary — the level when Deng's reforms began — it can.

Deng provided the economic incentives and the selection mechanism. The Compulsory
Education Law of 1986 formalised, in state law, what the CR had built informally in
village schools. The gaokao created the demand signal that made secondary education
valuable to families. These contributions were real. But the foundation was not his.

The college explosion belongs to Jiang Zemin, not Deng. And the mass literacy that made
any of this possible belongs to Mao — including, and especially, the decade that history
has labelled his greatest failure.

---

## Whose History Was Written

The "ten lost years" narrative was constructed by urban intellectuals who were personally
disrupted, Western journalists who could access that group, and the post-1978 Chinese
state, which needed to delegitimise the Cultural Revolution to justify Deng's break with
it. The international academic literature drew almost exclusively on urban memoirs and
official statistics that emphasised university closures, because that is what the people
writing the literature had experienced.

What was not recorded with equal weight: rural communities gaining their first secondary
school; girls in Sichuan villages learning to read; village health workers extending life
expectancy by a decade. The WCDE cohort reconstruction is, in a sense, the first time the
rural 80% have a quantitative voice in this debate. What they say, in percentage points, is
that the Cultural Revolution era was one of the fastest periods of educational expansion in
Chinese history — for the people who had previously had nothing.

That does not make the CR's violence acceptable, the persecution of intellectuals justified,
or the destruction of universities a good policy. It makes the label "educational catastrophe"
a category error. For the 2–3% who aspired to university it was a catastrophe. For the other
80% it was an expansion. And a society is not its elite.

The standard narrative is not wrong. It is just not about China.

## Data Notes

All completion rates are from WCDE v3, cohort reconstruction (script 02b). Cohort year
represents the year the cohort was approximately 20–24 (when they would have completed
secondary education). Pre-1980 estimates carry a modest upward bias from survivorship
effects, but the direction of all findings is robust to this. Life expectancy and TFR
are from the WCDE SSP2 historical series.
